In slow but steady steps

The challenge (and the ego blow) of running in Zone 2.

The Challenge of Running in Zone 2

Have you ever caught yourself trying to hide the reduced speed of your pace while an elderly lady running late overtakes you? If so, welcome to the Zone 2 club. That magical intensity range that promises miraculous transformations in your performance, but also threatens the integrity of your ego.

Zone 2 (Z2) is that calm and peaceful region where the body works with maximum oxygen without getting too tired. The ideal rhythm for it? Well, let's just say it's the one where you can blurt out a sentence complaining about something without turning purple from breathing too fast. Just to illustrate: imagine running at such a relaxed pace that you could chat with your friend, if they were willing to join you on this never-ending marathon.

Now, let's talk about the tricky part: why is it so hard to stick to Zone 2? Because it touches on things we don't like to admit: our lack of cardiovascular base and our fear of looking like "less of a runner" than we are. Here are some reasons why many runners struggle to adhere to Zone 2:

1. The Cardiovascular Mismatch

If you're a beginner or have the habit of always training at high intensity, your heart probably isn't used to working efficiently beat-by-beat. The result? Every time you try to run in Zone 2, the pace becomes so slow that you have to walk to stay in the right range. And oh, that day when your 5k training was supposed to be solid and planned, but ends up being a run interrupted by several involuntary walks.

2. The Ego Blow

Running in Zone 2 means maintaining a much slower pace than your usual maximum. When you see the speed dropping on your watch and running apps, a strange feeling takes over: it feels like you're becoming increasingly slow. The temptation is immediate: speed up to protect the ego and maintain that image of a determined runner.

3. Uncertain Calculations

Most people use the generic formula $220 - \text{age}$ to calculate their maximum heart rate, but that's like using a GPS that doesn't work properly in an unknown city. If the calculation underestimates your real max HR, Zone 2 becomes artificially low, and you find yourself running at a pace so slow that it's closer to walking than running.

4. The "No Pain, No Gain" Culture

There's a deep-rooted idea that training is only valid if it brings pain, suffering, and muscle exhaustion the next day. Zone 2, on the other hand, leaves you as fresh as when you started.

But there's the secret: training in Zone 2 is about patience and consistency. It's about understanding that you don't always need to run fast to improve; sometimes we just need to find a slower and more sustainable rhythm. And if that means watching several people pass you by, then welcome to the Zone 2 club — where progress is silent, but certain.

So, next time you're in doubt about your speed and feel that irrational urge to speed up, remember: running is not a race against time. It's a journey into your own potential — a potential that can be discovered at a slower pace than you imagine.

And if that makes you look like less of a runner... well, maybe it's time to redefine what it really means to be a runner.
